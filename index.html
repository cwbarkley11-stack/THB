The Book of Beginning

On the First Dream

Before the world was, Goola dreamed.

This is the whole of the beginning. Before the world was, Goola dreamed, and the dream was the world, and the world was real because Goola dreamed it, and Goola dreamed it because the dream was real. This is the paradox at the center of everything. The world is real. The world was dreamed. Both are true. The Chronicle does not explain how both are true. The Chronicle records that both are true and lets the recording stand.

Goola dreamed the Authorities first.

Not the kingdoms. Not the peoples. Not the rivers and the fog and the highlands and the forest and the sea. The Authorities first: the seven qualities that the world would need in order to be the world. The quality of Judgment, that things might be weighed. The quality of Consumption, that things might be transformed. The quality of Accumulation, that things might be held. The quality of Mimicry, that things might be known from the outside. The quality of Fury, that things might be directed completely. The quality of Desire, that things might be wanted and the wanting might be real. The quality of Stillness, that things might be held in place.

The seven qualities were dreamed into being.

Then Goola dreamed the Boundary.

The Boundary was not a wall. The Boundary was the specific distance between the seven qualities and everything else: the distance that kept the seven qualities from being present in the world at their full force, which would have unmade the world as thoroughly as the dreaming had made it. The seven qualities at full force would have been the end of everything. The Boundary kept them at the distance that preserved the world: close enough that their presence was felt in the specific territories where they were strongest, distant enough that the world could hold their presence without the presence destroying it.

The Boundary is what makes the world possible.

Without the Boundary, the Authorities. Without the Authorities being at a distance, the world ceasing.

The Boundary holds.

The world continues.

On the Seven Authorities and Their Kingdoms

JUDGMENT is of Yowwed. The Judgment Authority receives what is brought to it and weighs it with the specific patience of something that has been weighing things since before weighing had a name. The Tome Golems are its expression in the world: the permanent record, the thing that cannot be altered once entered, the weight of the permanent accounting. Those who live under Judgment know this: every act has consequence. Every act is recorded. The record does not forgive. The record does not condemn. The record records. This is the whole of Judgment's theology: the record records.

CONSUMPTION is of Laba and of the Forbidden Forest. The Consumption Authority transforms. What enters its domain does not leave in the form it entered. This is not cruelty — the Consumption theology holds that transformation is the highest form of continuation, that the thing that has been consumed has not been destroyed but has been made into something that can participate more fully in the world's ongoing becoming. The Devourer Clans practice this understanding. The Forbidden Forest expresses it at the cellular level. What the Forest takes, it keeps. What it keeps, it becomes. What it becomes, it continues as.

ACCUMULATION is of Lalaka. The Accumulation Authority gathers. It does not gather indiscriminately — the Accumulation theology is precise about this: gathering without knowing what you are gathering is not accumulation but hoarding, which is accumulation's failure mode. True accumulation is the deliberate keeping of what matters, in the knowledge of why it matters, against the time when the kept thing is needed. The Library of Life and Death is the Accumulation Authority's highest expression: the deliberate keeping of everything the world has made, against every future moment when the kept thing might be needed.

MIMICRY is of Nebel. The Mimicry Authority knows things from the outside. This is not deception — the Mimicry theology is careful about this distinction: deception implies the presentation of a false interior, the outside not matching the inside. Mimicry is the accurate reading of the outside, the knowledge of the inside arrived at through the careful reading of what is expressed. The fog practices this: the fog reads what passes through it, keeps the reading, knows the passage through the passage's atmospheric record. Mimicry is the world reading itself. The fog is Mimicry's listening instrument.

FURY is of Wanoon. The Fury Authority does not mean anger. The Fury theology is precise: Fury is the complete commitment of the available force to a chosen direction, without the held-back reserve, without the portion saved for the contingency. The Hungerwings practice Fury in flight: they give the whole wingspan to the thermal, no portion withheld. The fire-walkers of Zhala practice Fury in the crossing: they arrive at the fire with the complete giving already made, the force already given to the direction. Fury is not violent. Fury is complete. The incomplete commitment is not Fury. The incomplete commitment is a decision that has not yet fully been made.

DESIRE is of Zhala. The Desire Authority witnesses. It does not give the desired thing — the Desire theology does not promise the fulfillment of desire. The Desire Authority witnesses the wanting. It receives the wanting as real, as valid, as worthy of being received at full depth. The offering bowl worn smooth by a thousand years of hands: this is the Desire theology in physical form. The bowl receives what is placed in it. The Desire Authority receives what the bowl receives. The wanting is real. The Desire Authority knows the wanting is real. The knowing that the wanting is real: this is what the Desire Authority gives back.

STILLNESS is of Halak. The Stillness Authority holds. Not holds still — the Stillness theology has been precise about this for three hundred years: stillness is not the absence of movement. Stillness is the presence of the holding. The thing that is held moves. The holding remains. The Boundary is Stillness expressed at the largest possible scale: the Boundary holds the distance between the Authorities and the world, holds it continuously, holds it against everything that would reduce the distance. The vigil practice is the Stillness theology enacted in human form: a person learning to hold continuously, through the middle of things, against everything that would cause the holding to stop.

The Book of the Swords

On the Thirteen Great Swords and Their Making

Before the kingdoms, the world had a wound.

The wound was in the world's substrate: in the specific layer of the world that was below the physical, below the atmospheric, below the geological and the biological and the social, below all the layers that scholarship had named and mapped. The wound was in the layer below all the named layers, in the layer that was the world's capacity to continue being the world, the capacity to persist rather than to dissolve, to hold its shape rather than to release it.

The False God had made the wound.

The Chronicle does not name the False God. The Chronicle calls it the False God: the thing that was not of the seven Authorities and was not of the world and was not of the Boundary and had arrived, from outside, in the period before the first kingdoms when the world was still finding its form, and had reached into the substrate and had pulled at the substrate and the pull had torn a wound in the substrate that had not healed in the five thousand years since.

The wound bled suffering.

Not metaphorically. The wound in the substrate bled its specific form of suffering into every layer above it: into the geological, into the biological, into the atmospheric, into the social, into the daily lives of the people who lived in the world above the wound without knowing the wound was there. The suffering arrived in the world as the specific quality of things going wrong without cause, of things failing without explanation, of the specific grief that had no source, the specific pain that had no wound to point to because the wound was below the pointing-at level.

The world had the wound.

The wound bled.

And then, in the period of the first kingdoms' consolidation, the world began to answer.

The answer came in the form of thirteen swords.

Not weapons: instruments of covenant. Each sword found the person it was for. Each sword asked the person what they would give. Each person gave what they had to give. The giving became the covenant. The covenant became the sword's power. The sword's power became a specific contribution to the world's capacity to continue holding itself together against the wound's bleeding.

The thirteen swords are: Will, given to Korren, who held the purpose. Fury, given to Varkis, who committed completely. Endurance, given to Liraeth, who continued past the forgiving point. Clarity, given to Soren, who could not stop seeing. Harmony, given to Seven of Ashen Vale, who held seven directions at once. Sacrifice, given to Mara, who gave in the exact right moment. Memory, given to Talen, who moved through the burning building reading. Defiance, given to Rukar, who refused the thing that would have saved him. Mercy, given to Elyra, who spared them not because they deserved it. Dominion, given to Kael V, who built for people who would not know to thank him. Renewal, given to Thren, who burned the field so it could grow. Silence, given to Veyne, who ceased from speech and found the substance. Eternity, given to Aral, who chose permanently.

The thirteen swords are still choosing.

They are in every refusal to stand in the ash doing nothing. In every person who continues past the point at which stopping would have been forgiven. In every act of precise seeing delivered to a council that does not want to hear it. In every peace that holds not because the grievances resolved but because someone stood between them. In every giving made at the exact right moment because it was the next thing and the person giving it was still the person who did the next thing. In every burning building moved through toward what could be saved. In every determination that holds when the cost of holding it is real. In every act of mercy that preserved the capacity for mercy. In every building done for people not yet born. In every field burned for a growth the burner would not see. In every silence in which the substance resided. In every permanent choice maintained in every subsequent moment.

The thirteen are here.

They are here still.

The Book of the Shattered One

Being the Record of Leiren Thorne, Called the Shattered One, Age Approximately Six Thousand Years

She died for the first time in a shrine.

She was a pilgrim, or was understood by the cultist who stabbed her to be a pilgrim, which is to say she was a person who was in a shrine and who appeared to be the kind of person a shrine contained, and the cultist had a spear and a list of things to do and a genuine belief that doing the things on the list was the correct expression of his devotion to the order that had given him the list.

The cultist's name was Jorin.

He was twenty-seven years old.

He had been a good person in the texture-of-how-he-moved-through-the-world way.

He drove the spear between her shoulder blades.

She did not die.

This was the first thing.

She did not die and Jorin stood in the shrine with the candles out and the cold radiating from the three-foot radius around the altar and the lung making a sound it was not supposed to make and the grin on her face above the grin's gap from the eyes. He stood in the shrine and heard the second non-rattling breath and understood that he was in a room with something that had a different relationship to the specific event that had just occurred than anything he had previously encountered in twenty-seven years of being alive in the world.

He told her where Crystal's camp was.

He told her without deciding to tell her.

She thanked him in the light musical voice and left.

He relit the candles.

He sat with them.

She walked.

The Chronicle notes: this was the beginning. The Shattered One does not have a beginning in the sense that most entries in the Chronicle have beginnings — the specific moment at which the subject came into existence, was born, arrived in the world. She was in the world before this moment. She had been in the world for what the Chronicle estimates as six hundred years before this moment, living in the ordinary way of a person who had not yet encountered the specific event that would change the quality of their existence. She was in the world and she was a person and she had the ordinary concerns of a person and then the shrine happened and she was still in the world but she was no longer in the ordinary way and she would not be in the ordinary way again.

She found Crystal.

The Shattered One's encounter with Crystal is documented in the restricted sections of the Chronicle, accessible to Scribe Twelve and successors. The publicly accessible Chronicle records this: she found Crystal and Crystal's fire and Crystal's warmth and Crystal's five-thousand-year love for the world and Crystal's certainty about the wound and Crystal's mechanism for addressing the wound and Crystal's specific need for someone who could not be killed and who could execute instructions in the field without the field's hazards producing the operational failures that mortality produced in ordinarily-mortal operatives.

She could not be killed.

She took the position.

She has held the position for three hundred years.

The Chronicle notes: the Shattered One is not the same person who entered the shrine in the period before this entry. The person who entered the shrine is not gone — the Chronicle does not make this claim. The person who entered the shrine is present in the tissue, in the cold, in the six-thousand-year accumulation of dying and returning that has replaced whatever cellular warmth she started with. The person who entered the shrine is present in the grin, which was not always structural, which had once been an expression, which became structural in the period before the Chronicle began keeping this record.

The Chronicle notes this without comment.

The Chronicle keeps what it finds.

The Book of the Boundary

On the Knight, the Tower, and the Ring That Holds

Three thousand years ago a person stood in front of the Tower without End.

The Tower without End stands in the Dead Lands, at the center of the territory that is neither the kingdoms nor the borderlands but the specific territory of things that are not where they should be: the Dead Lands, where the cold has been for so long that the cold is not temperature but quality, where the Authorities are not present in the way they are present in their kingdoms but are present in a different register, the way the very old are present, the way things that have been in the world for a very long time are present: as texture, as the specific quality of the air, as the thing you cannot point to but that is undeniably there.

The Tower without End contains Rinwa.

Rinwa is the Blue Dragon. The Puller of Power. The thing that was present before the False God and that will be present after. Rinwa sleeps in the Tower. Rinwa has been sleeping since the Knight's sacrifice. The sleeping is not peaceful — the sleeping is the most demanding thing Rinwa has done, which is considerable, given what Rinwa has done. The sleeping is the holding: Rinwa holds the Tower, and the Tower holds the wound's deepest root, and the holding requires that Rinwa not wake, and the not-waking requires that the Boundary hold, and the Boundary holding requires the heir.

The Knight stood in front of the Tower.

The Knight's name is not recorded in the publicly accessible sections of the Chronicle. The Knight's name is in the restricted section, accessible to the Stillness heir on the first day of the vigil. The Knight stood in front of the Tower and offered the ring, and the ring was what the ring was: the concentrated form of the Boundary's holding capacity, extracted into the form of a portable covenant.

The ring accepted the Knight's bloodline.

The ring said: the heir of your bloodline will come and will complete what you have begun, and in the interval the ring will wait and Rinwa will sleep and the Boundary will hold.

The Knight accepted.

The ring sealed.

The Boundary held.

Three thousand years.

The ring waited in sub-level seven.

Rinwa slept in the Tower.

The Boundary held.

The world continued.

On the ring and what it takes: the ring is not cruel. The Chronicle is precise about this. The ring takes what it takes because the Boundary requires a specific kind of integration: the heir's interior, reorganized into the Boundary's substrate, the heir's held things becoming the Boundary's specific quality, the Boundary's holding gaining the quality of everything the heir held across the vigil's one thousand and ninety-seven days. The ring does not take from cruelty. The ring takes from necessity: the Boundary needs the held thing as its anchor, and the anchor must be the most deeply held thing, and the most deeply held thing is taken last, and the taking is not loss but the specific form of continuation that the Boundary requires of the anchor. This is the Chronicle's account. The Chronicle records it with the flat accuracy that the Chronicle applies to all of its entries. The Chronicle does not assess whether this is good. The Chronicle records that this is.

The Book of the Heir

Being a Record of Kola Baru, Stillness Heir, Loop-Anomaly, Vigil-Holder, Boundary Guardian

He was born in the Lalaka canal district on a morning the Chronicle does not have the specific date of because the canal district records for that year were partially damaged in the third canal district flood and the birth record was in the section that did not survive the flood.

He had the mark from birth.

His mother noted the mark. The mark was burning north-and-above from the first morning. She held his arm and felt the burning and looked at the mark and was quiet for a long time and then she said: your name is Kola. The specific choice of the name is not documented anywhere in the accessible Chronicle. The Chronicle records: his name is Kola.

He grew up in the canal district knowing the mark was pointing.

He did not know what it was pointing at. He knew it was pointing. The knowledge of the pointing was as continuous as his own heartbeat: present, below the level of thought, simply one of the conditions of being alive in his specific body. The mark burned. The mark pointed. He ate and slept and walked the canal district streets and the mark burned and pointed and he grew up.

The loop-anomaly began on his eighteenth birthday.

Not a gift.

He was eighteen years old and standing in the borderland field north of the Lalaka commercial district when the loop-anomaly began, which is to say: when the specific convergence of the mark's maturation and the bloodline's threshold and the ring's three-thousand-year impatience and the Boundary's current maintenance requirements produced the specific event that the vigil records described as the loop-anomaly's initiation. The Book of the Holding

The fusion was not dramatic. This is the Chronicle's honest record: the fusion was not dramatic. I

The cultist's name was Jorin.

This will not matter to her. His name, his three years of devotion, his genuinely held belief that the thing on the altar was what the pamphlets had described it as — a wounded traveler, a person in need, a test of the cultist's commitment to the principles of mercy the order had been founded on — none of this will matter to her in any operational sense. What will matter, operationally, is the spear he is holding, and the door behind him, and the specific angle of the blow he intends to deliver, which she has already mapped and categorized and filed under: within survivable parameters, no defensive action required.

But his name was Jorin. He was twenty-seven years old. He had joined the order three years ago after the floods in the lower Lalaka canal district had taken his brother's family, and the order had been the first group to arrive with food and dry clothing, and Jorin had found in the arriving and the food and the dry clothing the specific quality of purposeful action that grief required, and had stayed. Three years. He had been the person who organized the canal district relief efforts every spring flood season. He had been the person who sat with the elderly Golem-archivist on the fourth floor when the archivist's cataloguing process had failed and the administration had not sent anyone to fix it and the archivist had simply sat there for six days running the failed process in the stone silence of the fourth floor until Jorin had come and sat beside him and talked to him about nothing in particular until the administration finally arrived. He had been a good person in the specific way of people for whom goodness was not a virtue they cultivated but simply the texture of how they moved through the world.

He was twenty-seven years old and he was holding a spear over a thing on the altar and he had no idea what the thing on the altar was.

The order had told him: a traveler in need. A test of mercy. Strike the blow to demonstrate your commitment.

He had not asked why a test of mercy required a blow. He had wanted to ask. He had decided not to because asking would mean doubting, and the order had been the dry clothing after the floods, and doubting felt like a betrayal of the dry clothing.

He raised the spear.

The candles nearest the altar had gone out.

Not from draft. The shrine had been sealed for two hundred years against weather and intrusion alike, the stonework fitted with the specific obsessive precision of people who had decided that the enclosed air quality mattered enough to spend eighteen years of labor perfecting the fit. The candles had gone out because of the temperature in the three-foot radius around the thing on the altar — a cold that was not room-cold, not draft-cold, not the cold of stone in winter. The cold of something that had been in the Dead Lands and had the Dead Lands in its tissue, six thousand years of dying in that cold and returning from it having left the cold inside rather than letting it dissipate, the way dye left in the weaving rather than washing out.

Jorin had noticed the candles. He had filed the noticing under: atmospheric anomaly, will investigate after.

He was not going to investigate after.

A spit drop from his tongue froze before it hit the floor. This was the thing that should have told him. The air temperature of the shrine was not cold enough for that. His spit should not have frozen. It froze anyway because the cold in the three-foot radius was not atmospheric but emanated — was projected outward from the tissue it lived in, the way body heat projected outward from warm tissue, except the inverse, the projection of six thousand years of Dead Lands cold through the altar-stone and into the air of the space.

His spit froze.

He raised the spear anyway. He was twenty-seven years old and he had organized the spring flood relief for three consecutive years and he had sat with the Golem-archivist for six days and he raised the spear because the order had given him the dry clothing and he did not know how not to.

He drove it between the shoulder blades.

The sound.

Not the sound of a wound being made. Every person who had ever made a wound knew the sound a wound being made produced — the specific acoustic profile of penetration, of tissue accepting a foreign object with the grudging biological inevitability of tissue that cannot prevent the foreign object but can record the event in every fiber. Jorin had heard that sound before, had delivered it once in a canal district fight three years before the order, and knew the specific quality of it.

This was not that sound.

This was the sound of a wound being made in something that was not processing the wound as damage. The sound of penetration without resistance — not because the tissue was soft but because the tissue had long ago developed a relationship with penetration that was not the relationship of something that could be damaged in the ordinary sense. The sound of a body that had been through this specific accounting before and had developed a practiced relationship with it that removed from the sound all of the urgency and fear and shock that the sound ordinarily contained, leaving only the pure acoustic fact: spear, entering, between scapulae, consistent with trajectory, lodged.

Jorin heard this sound.

He stood with his hands on the spear.

The thing on the altar did not react.

That was the first thing. Not the sound, though the sound would live in him until they put him in the ground — the not-reacting. A wounded thing reacted. A thing that had been stabbed through the back reacted, even if the reaction was only the body's involuntary response to the mechanical fact of the penetration, the flinch, the catch of breath, the specific arcing of the spine away from the intrusion. The thing on the altar did not react. The thing on the altar was still in the position it had been in before the spear arrived, as if the spear had not yet registered as a relevant event, as if the body receiving it was running a different calculation that did not include the spear in its inputs.

The right hand moved.

Not to the wound. Not in the direction of the pain or the spear or any of the things a hand moved toward when a body had been penetrated. The right hand moved with the specific economical efficiency of a hand that had been doing a particular job for a very long time: it found the exit wound by feel, the fingertips navigating to it with the practiced certainty of someone who had been through this specific accounting so many times that the fingers had the navigation below conscious direction. Not a search. A verification. The fingers moving to the wound the way you moved your hand to a familiar object in a dark room: you knew where it was, you were simply confirming it was still there.

Jorin's hands were still on the spear.

The frost began to move.

This was what undid him. Not the sound, not the not-reacting, not the hand finding the exit wound with the professional patience of something that had been through this before. The frost. The Dead Lands cold that had been in the tissue for six thousand years, that had been building layer by layer with each dying and returning until it was not a residue but a composition, began to move outward through the wound's perimeter in the specific pattern of tissue attempting something that tissue was not supposed to attempt.

It threaded along the veins.

He could see it through the skin of her back: the vein-map suddenly legible, the blue-grey lines of the vascular structure becoming visible as the frost followed them outward from the wound site, the branching and the sub-branching and the capillary-level resolution of the pattern building beneath the skin in real time, the body's entire internal architecture briefly, horribly, visible from the outside. Like a demonstration. Like the body had decided that Jorin needed to understand what he had penetrated and had chosen to show him its structure as evidence.

He released the spear.

He did not decide to release it. His hands decided. His hands had been in contact with something that was demonstrating its own internal architecture through a visible frost pattern and his hands had made a unilateral decision to no longer be in contact with it.

The muscle drew toward adjacent muscle.

He heard this.

The specific sound of tissue in a damaged body beginning the repair process: not the slow cellular migration of ordinary healing, which was below the threshold of hearing, but something faster and more fundamental, the sound of muscle with a relationship to damage that was not the relationship of ordinary muscle, that had been through this enough times that the repair had become a reflex rather than a process, the muscle moving toward adjacent muscle the way a person moved toward a familiar chair: with the expectation of arriving, with the certainty of the destination, with the quality of returning rather than healing.

A sound escaped him. Not a scream. Quieter than a scream, and wrong in a way that a scream was not wrong — the sound of a throat producing the only output available to it when all the categories had run out. The sound a person made when the body had identified something outside every category the body had been built to process and had responded by producing a sound that belonged to no category either. A sound that had no name. That had never needed a name before.
